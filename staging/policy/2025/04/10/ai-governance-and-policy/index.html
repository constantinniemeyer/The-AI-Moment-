AI Governance and Policy: The Global Race to Regulate Artificial Intelligence

How the EU, US, China, and global bodies are attempting to govern the most consequential technology of our time

Source: EU AI Act — Official Text

Artificial intelligence governance has become one of the most contested areas of technology policy. Governments across the world are racing to establish rules for AI development and deployment — navigating between the desire to capture AI’s economic benefits and the imperative to manage its risks.

The EU AI Act

The European Union’s AI Act, which entered into force in 2024, is the world’s most comprehensive AI regulation to date. It takes a risk-based approach:

  • Unacceptable risk (banned): AI systems that manipulate people subconsciously, use real-time biometric surveillance in public, enable social scoring by governments
  • High risk (strictly regulated): AI in critical infrastructure, medical devices, law enforcement, education assessment, credit scoring, border control
  • Limited risk: Transparency obligations — chatbots must disclose they are AI; deepfakes must be labelled
  • Minimal risk: No regulation — spam filters, game-playing AI, etc.

Frontier AI models (those trained above a compute threshold) face additional obligations around capability testing, transparency, and safety.

The United States Approach

The US has taken a lighter-touch, sectoral approach. A 2023 Executive Order on AI directed federal agencies to develop AI safety standards, required developers of powerful AI to share safety test results with the government, and established AI safety institutes within NIST and the Department of Homeland Security.

US policy has been characterised by:

  • Emphasis on maintaining competitive advantage over China
  • Reliance on voluntary commitments from leading AI companies
  • Sector-specific rules (FDA for medical AI, financial regulators for fintech AI)
  • Debate between Congress and the administration over the need for comprehensive legislation

China’s Approach

China has moved quickly to regulate specific AI applications while supporting domestic AI development as a strategic priority. Rules covering:

  • Algorithmic recommendation systems (2022)
  • Deepfakes and synthetic media (2022)
  • Generative AI services (2023)

China’s approach requires AI systems to embody “core socialist values” and restricts content that threatens national unity — reflecting a governance model that prioritises social stability alongside safety.

Global Coordination

Multilateral efforts at AI governance are nascent but growing:

  • The OECD AI Principles (2019) established broad normative commitments adopted by 46 countries
  • The Bletchley Declaration (2023) — signed at the UK’s AI Safety Summit — established consensus that frontier AI poses potentially catastrophic risks requiring international cooperation
  • The UN has established an AI advisory body and is exploring treaty options
  • The G7 Hiroshima AI Process produced a set of voluntary guiding principles for advanced AI

The Core Tensions

AI governance involves genuine tensions that resist easy resolution:

Innovation vs. Safety: Regulation imposes compliance costs and slows deployment. The EU AI Act’s supporters argue it will build the trust necessary for sustainable adoption; critics argue it will disadvantage European AI companies.

National Competition vs. Global Coordination: Effective governance of frontier AI requires international cooperation, but AI leadership is also a strategic geopolitical asset that nations are reluctant to constrain.

Precaution vs. Evidence: Some risks (existential from superintelligent AI) are speculative but potentially catastrophic; others (current harms from biased systems) are documented but contested in severity. Different stakeholders weight these differently.

Transparency vs. Intellectual Property: Meaningful safety oversight requires insight into how systems work; AI developers argue their architectures and training data are proprietary.

What to Watch

The next phase of AI governance will be shaped by:

  • Whether the EU AI Act’s enforcement proves effective
  • Whether US Congress passes comprehensive AI legislation
  • How AI liability law develops (who is responsible when AI causes harm?)
  • The outcome of the global discussion about AI safety standards for frontier models

AI governance is not a technical problem with a technical solution. It is fundamentally a political question about who gets to benefit from AI, who bears its risks, and who has the authority to decide.
